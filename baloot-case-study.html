UX Case Study • Baloot Micro‑Saving Platform

Baloot – building trust in micro‑savings and investment

Baloot is an Iranian micro‑saving and investment platform that helps people preserve the value of their money despite high inflation. Over one and a half years, we designed and iterated on the product across research, UX, UI and design system – with a focus on automation, trust and accessibility for users with low financial literacy.

01 · Overview
Problem & product goal

Problem statement

Many people intend to save a portion of their monthly income for future goals but struggle to do so consistently. They often forget to put money aside, or more importantly, fear that inflation will erode the value of their savings over time. Without simple, trustworthy tools, saving and investing remains complex, risky and emotionally stressful.

Baloot’s goal is to remove this friction: help users save and invest even small amounts of money, automatically or manually, while preserving their purchasing power in valid, stable investment funds and stocks.

Product goal

Make micro‑savings and investment effortless and trustworthy:

  • Allow users to save small amounts automatically on a daily, weekly or monthly basis.
  • Keep their savings invested in stable funds and stocks to stay ahead of inflation.
  • Provide a clear, simple interface for users with low financial literacy.
  • Build trust through transparency, education and strong institutional partnerships.
02 · Design process
From research to evaluation

Approach

Over ~1.5 years, the team followed a user‑centered, iterative process:

  • Empathy & discovery – user interviews, surveys, competitive analysis.
  • Ideation – user flows, information architecture, card sorting, brainstorming.
  • Design – sketches, wireframes, visual design, illustration, interaction design, design system.
  • Evaluation – moderated usability tests, surveys, interviews, and data analysis.
  • Implementation support – design‑dev handoff, coordination and iteration.

Methods used

User interviews Surveys Competitive analysis Card sorting Usability testing Data analysis (4,895+ samples)
Data sources
We collected behavioral and attitudinal data from social media (Instagram, LinkedIn) and lifestyle influencers, resulting in 4,895 data points across income and occupation segments – with at least 115 responses per segment and over 400 in most, ensuring reliable insights.
03 · Research insights
Pain points, needs & trust

Key interview questions

We explored users’ mental models around saving and investing:

  • How did you start investing, from first interest to first investment?
  • How familiar are you with stock market concepts and investment methods?
  • Why do you save and why do you invest? What percentage of your income?
  • How do you research financial topics and which devices do you use?
  • How do you evaluate and trust investment applications?

Pain points

Lack of financial information Instability of financial markets Fear of losing savings Low income & small amounts Lack of trust in investment methods Complex financial apps

User needs

  • Invest with small amounts of money, without advanced knowledge.
  • Protect the financial value of their money from inflation.
  • Simple, guided flows and explanations for funds and investment options.
  • Insurance‑like safety around value stability.
  • Automatic saving so they don’t need to remember every time.

Why people save

Motivations vary by income level and life stage, but common reasons include:

  • Buying or renting a house (especially for incomes above 8–12M).
  • Travel and migration.
  • Children’s expenses and education.
  • Unpredictable incidents and emergencies.
  • Buying personal items.

Trust in investment apps

Trust levels in savings/investment apps are mostly medium:

  • Users with no familiarity with finance show the highest distrust.
  • Users who are “completely proficient” are polarized – very trusting or very distrustful.
  • Getting to know financial and investment topics has a direct relationship with app trust.
Opportunity: educating users and increasing transparency are key levers to build trust in Baloot.
04 · Personas & journey
Who we design for

Primary persona

Instead of multiple separate personas, we created a comprehensive persona that captures common traits:

“I want to invest for my future, but I don’t know where to start.”
  • 18+ years old, typically middle‑class.
  • Low financial education and low to medium income.
  • Lazy or forgetful about saving and investing.
  • Feels small amounts are “not worth” investing.
  • Struggles with complex UI in financial apps.
  • Worries about losing savings and doesn’t fully trust apps.
  • Wants simple, automatic investment and basic financial literacy training.
  • Goals include preserving money value, investing for children, and accessing services like life insurance and cashback.

User segments by risk & knowledge

Baloot users fall into four broad groups:

  1. Low income, low knowledge, low–moderate risk tolerance (often students).
  2. Low income & low knowledge; mainly low to moderate investment level.
  3. Medium risk‑taking, income from low to high, investment from low to high.
  4. Intermediate to high stock‑market knowledge, moderate to high income and investment.

Groups 3 and 4 generally have higher confidence in markets and are more risk‑taking. Baloot supports both users who want automated investment and those who prefer manual control.

High‑level journey

Stimulus
Inflation & unstable economy

Severe inflation, unstable economic situation, fear of decreasing money value, exposure to social‑network trading and fraud push users to look for safer ways to save.

Discovery
Seeking trustworthy options

Users ask friends, follow financial influencers, compare banks, funds, brokerage and digital currency options; they feel confused by contradictory information and low financial awareness.

Onboarding
First contact with Baloot

They reach Baloot via website, social media, Google ads or partner agencies, then go through app onboarding, legal verification and account setup. Registration complexity, unfamiliar concepts and infrastructure issues can create friction.

Saving & investing
Activating automatic and manual flows

Users explore automatic saving, automatic investment (smart / periodic) and manual fund purchase. Clear guidance, transparent status and smooth flows are critical for trust and repeat use.

05 · Solution
Key flows & main pages

Automatic investment: periodic & smart

One of the most important UX challenges was connecting automatic savings with actual investment. In the old version, many users activated automatic saving but never moved to investment.

New model

  • Periodic investment – users choose their bank, investment fund, amount and time interval; Baloot automatically withdraws and invests on the chosen schedule.
  • Smart investment – users activate automatic saving and select a target fund and units. When the wallet balance reaches the price of one unit, Baloot automatically buys it.
Design decision: automatic saving became a tool for automatic investment, not a separate prerequisite. This reduced dead‑end states and aligned flows with user expectations.

Core scenarios

  1. Automatic investment – select auto‑invest, read onboarding, choose smart/periodic, pick fund, amount and bank, confirm.
  2. Automatic saving – open auto saving, adjust default amount and interval, choose bank, connect to direct debit.
  3. Manual investment – pick a fund, review information, choose units or amount, pay via banking gateway.

Main app pages

  • Home – portfolio value, account balance, “next step” guidance card, banners for auto saving / auto investment / fund purchase, booklet, invite friends.
  • Automatic investment – configure smart vs periodic investment, view current settings and edit easily.
  • Manual & quick fund purchase – choose account and fund, invest directly; clear entry points to automatic investment.
  • Automatic saving – enable recurring saving from a chosen bank; reused funds for automatic or manual investing.
  • My account – balance, withdrawals, bills, recent cash‑deposit profit, entry points to automatic saving and investment.
  • Future prediction – forecast charts that show how savings and investments grow over time, based on user age and auto‑investment settings.
06 · Trust & ecosystem
Partners, guidance & safety nets

Building trust

Because trust is central to investment behavior, we designed multiple reinforcing mechanisms:

  • Institutional partnerships – cooperation with licensed brokerages (e.g. Sahm Ashena, Mehrafrin) under supervision of the Securities and Exchange Organization.
  • Bank network – integration with 15+ banks (Bank Mellat, Melli, Keshavarzi, Ayandeh, etc.) and Central Bank oversight for automated savings.
  • Transparent documentation – contracts and certificates of deposit are visible in‑app for every user.
  • Guided onboarding – onboarding slides, contextual help icons and a “Booklet” section explain concepts and flows in simple language.

Complementary features

To support long‑term loyalty and more holistic financial behavior, Baloot explores:

  • Life insurance – integrated flow to choose duration, payment method and beneficiaries.
  • Cashback – contracts with partners (like Takhfifan) so part of card‑payment commission is automatically invested for users.
  • Baloot card & account – debit card linked to Baloot account, enabling round‑up savings, instant withdrawals and simpler everyday payments.

User behavior breakdown

  • 29% – use both automatic saving and automatic investment.
  • 49% – invest manually only.
  • 17% – use automatic saving only.
  • 5% – save automatically and also purchase funds manually.
72.14% of users who have automatic savings also continue to invest automatically (smart or periodic), showing a strong shift toward set‑and‑forget behavior once trust and flows are clear.

Competitor positioning

Compared to competitors like Daran and Kian Digital, Baloot differentiates through:

  • Automatic investment and automatic savings as first‑class features.
  • Flexible options for various risk levels and stable funds.
  • A UI tailored specifically to user pain points and mental models.

While some competitors focus more on advice, risk analysis or commodity funds, Baloot focuses on practical automation and simplicity within an unstable economic environment, where online risk scoring may not be reliable.

Design system
Visual language

Color palette

#593BE2 Primary purple
#2F179B Deep accent
#1BC47D Success green
#FFB958 Warm highlight

The visual language uses soft neutrals (#FFFFFF to #1E1E1E), purple and green accents from the original design (#DAD3F8, #C7BCF5, #593BE2, #2F179B, #128756, #1BC47D, #A5F3D2), plus warm tones (#B36800, #FFB958, #FFE3A0, #FFF1D1) for financial highlights.

Typography & components

  • Inspired by the original Baloot design system (Vazir family, multiple weights).
  • Hierarchy from Heading 1–5, tuned for readability on mobile and desktop.
  • Button variants for primary, secondary, outline and critical (red) actions.
  • Bottom sheets, snackbars and keyboard treatment to reduce cognitive load.
Usability testing
What we improved

Moderated usability tests with 24 participants uncovered issues in navigation, clarity of balances, discoverability of manual and automatic investment, and crowded information on transaction pages.

  • Added a “Your next step on Baloot” card to guide users toward account completion and investment.
  • Created the “Booklet” and inline help icons to explain complex concepts.
  • Removed a confusing “my account” progress bar and clarified labels and contrast.
  • Redesigned withdrawal cards with clearer hierarchy, color‑coded statuses and progressive disclosure.
  • Simplified automatic saving settings and split time/amount into separate, easier steps.
